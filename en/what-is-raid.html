What Is RAID? A Beginner-Friendly Guide

RAID is a way to use multiple drives together to improve storage capacity, speed, and fault tolerance. It is commonly used in NAS systems, servers, and custom-built PCs.

What Is RAID?

RAID stands for "Redundant Array of Independent Disks." It is a technology that combines multiple HDDs or SSDs into a single logical unit. Depending on the RAID level, it can increase storage capacity, improve read/write speed, and provide protection against drive failure.

Why Use RAID?

  • More capacity: Combine multiple drives into one larger storage space
  • Better speed: Some RAID levels can improve read and write performance
  • Higher fault tolerance: Some RAID levels can keep data accessible even if a drive fails

Common RAID Levels

RAID 0

RAID 0 splits data across multiple drives. It offers high speed and full capacity efficiency, but if even one drive fails, all data is lost.

RAID 1

RAID 1 mirrors the same data across multiple drives. It offers strong redundancy, but usable capacity is reduced because the data is duplicated.

RAID 5

RAID 5 distributes both data and parity information across multiple drives. It provides a good balance of capacity efficiency and fault tolerance, making it popular for home NAS setups.

RAID 6

RAID 6 is similar to RAID 5 but offers higher redundancy. It can tolerate up to two drive failures, though usable capacity is lower.

RAID 10

RAID 10 combines RAID 1 and RAID 0. It offers both high performance and redundancy, but it requires more drives.

Recommended RAID Levels by Use Case

  • Home NAS: RAID 1 or RAID 5
  • Data safety first: RAID 1 or RAID 6
  • Performance-focused workloads: RAID 0 or RAID 10
  • Balanced choice: RAID 5

RAID Is Not a Backup

RAID can protect against drive failure, but it does not protect you from accidental deletion, malware, ransomware, fire, theft, or other disasters. Important data should always be backed up separately, such as to an external drive or cloud storage.
