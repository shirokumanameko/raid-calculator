What Is RAID? A Beginner-Friendly Guide

RAID is a technology that combines multiple disks to improve capacity, performance, and fault tolerance. It is commonly used in NAS systems, servers, and custom-built PCs.

What Is RAID?

RAID stands for "Redundant Array of Independent Disks". It allows multiple HDDs or SSDs to be treated as a single storage system. With RAID, you can increase capacity, improve read/write performance, and enhance data protection against disk failures.

Why Use RAID?

  • Increase capacity: Combine multiple disks into one large storage pool
  • Improve performance: Distribute data for faster read/write speeds
  • Enhance reliability: Some RAID levels protect against disk failure

Common RAID Levels

RAID 0

Data is striped across multiple disks. It offers high performance and full capacity usage, but no redundancy—if one disk fails, all data is lost.

RAID 1

Data is mirrored across disks. It provides strong data protection, but usable capacity is reduced.

RAID 5

Data and parity are distributed across disks. It offers a good balance of performance, capacity, and redundancy, making it popular for home NAS systems.

RAID 6

Similar to RAID 5 but with double parity, allowing up to two disk failures. Usable capacity is lower.

RAID 10

A combination of RAID 1 and RAID 0. It provides both performance and redundancy, but requires more disks.

Which RAID Should You Choose?

  • Maximize capacity: RAID 0
  • High data safety: RAID 1 or RAID 6
  • Balanced option: RAID 5
  • Performance + safety: RAID 10

RAID Is Not a Backup

RAID protects against hardware failure, but it does not protect against accidental deletion, malware, ransomware, or disasters. Always keep a separate backup of important data.
