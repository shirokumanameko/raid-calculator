RAID Capacity Calculation Guide

The usable capacity of a RAID array depends on the RAID level, the number of disks, and the size of each disk. This page explains how to calculate RAID capacity for RAID 0, RAID 1, RAID 5, RAID 6, and RAID 10 in a simple way.

If you want to understand the basics first, see What Is RAID?.

RAID Capacity Basics

In RAID, not all raw disk capacity is always available for storing data. Some RAID levels use part of the total capacity for mirroring or parity, which reduces usable capacity but improves redundancy.

RAID 0 Capacity

RAID 0 uses all disk capacity for data.

Formula: Number of disks × Disk size

Example: 4 disks × 8TB = 32TB usable

RAID 1 Capacity

RAID 1 mirrors data, so the usable capacity is equal to one disk.

Formula: Capacity of one disk

Example: 2 disks × 8TB = 8TB usable

RAID 5 Capacity

RAID 5 uses the capacity of one disk for parity.

Formula: (Number of disks - 1) × Disk size

Example: 4 disks × 8TB = 24TB usable

RAID 6 Capacity

RAID 6 uses the capacity of two disks for parity.

Formula: (Number of disks - 2) × Disk size

Example: 4 disks × 8TB = 16TB usable

RAID 10 Capacity

RAID 10 combines mirroring and striping, so usable capacity is half of the total.

Formula: (Number of disks / 2) × Disk size

Example: 4 disks × 8TB = 16TB usable

Quick Comparison

  • RAID 0: Maximum capacity, no redundancy
  • RAID 1: High redundancy, low capacity efficiency
  • RAID 5: Good balance of capacity and redundancy
  • RAID 6: Better fault tolerance, lower usable capacity
  • RAID 10: Good performance and redundancy, needs more disks

Example: 4 × 8TB Disks

  • RAID 0 → 32TB
  • RAID 1 → 8TB
  • RAID 5 → 24TB
  • RAID 6 → 16TB
  • RAID 10 → 16TB
